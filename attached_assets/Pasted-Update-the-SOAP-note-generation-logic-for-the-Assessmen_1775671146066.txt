Update the SOAP note generation logic for the **Assessment & Plan** so that it is more clinically substantive and less dry/template-like.

## Goal

I want the Assessment & Plan to read like a thoughtful medical note written by a clinician who is synthesizing the visit, not just listing diagnoses with one-line plans. It should still remain accurate, medically appropriate, and strictly grounded in the encounter data, patient history, symptoms discussed, available labs, medications, and any linked prior findings.

## What I want changed

### 1) Add an Assessment Summary paragraph

Inside the **Assessment** section, before the diagnosis-by-diagnosis list begins, insert a short clinical summary paragraph.

This paragraph should:

* briefly synthesize the overall clinical picture
* reflect the main concerns discussed during the visit
* connect symptoms, relevant history, trends, or labs when appropriate
* sound medically thoughtful and professional
* avoid fluff, overstatement, or invented facts

### 2) Make each Assessment/Plan item more substantive

For each diagnosis/problem, I want the note to include a little more medical substance.

Instead of:

* diagnosis
* one brief generic plan line

I want:

* concise clinical reasoning
* relevant context from the encounter
* pertinent symptom/lab/medication linkage when available
* rationale for the treatment or next step
* follow-up/monitoring language when appropriate

The tone should still be concise and chart-appropriate, but not overly bare or robotic.

## Important guardrails

Do **not** invent symptoms, exam findings, labs, diagnoses, counseling, or treatment decisions that were not actually supported by:

* the transcript
* structured intake data
* linked labs
* medication list
* known problem list
* clinician-selected diagnoses/orders

If information is missing, the note should stay appropriately general rather than making things up.

## Desired style

The output should sound:

* medical
* professional
* clinically reasoned
* concise but meaningful
* similar to how a real NP/PA/MD would summarize their thinking in a polished outpatient note

It should **not** sound:

* generic
* repetitive
* padded
* overly academic
* overly verbose
* like AI filler

## Preferred structure example

### Assessment

[Assessment Summary Paragraph Here]

1. [Diagnosis]

   * Brief clinical interpretation of the issue in the context of this visit.
   * Relevant symptoms/history/lab correlation if applicable.
   * Plan: medication management, counseling, monitoring, follow-up, referrals, or testing as appropriate.

2. [Diagnosis]

   * Same style.

## Example of tone difference

### Too dry:

Hyperlipidemia.
Will continue lifestyle changes. Repeat labs in 3 months.

### Better:

Hyperlipidemia with persistent elevation in atherogenic markers on prior review. We discussed ongoing dietary efforts and the importance of long-term risk reduction, particularly in the setting of her broader metabolic picture. Will continue lifestyle intervention at this time and repeat lipid-related markers with follow-up labs to reassess trajectory and determine whether additional treatment is warranted.

### Too dry:

Perimenopause.
Continue current medications.

### Better:

Perimenopausal symptom pattern remains consistent with ongoing hormonal fluctuation, with continued concerns including sleep disruption, mood variability, and daytime fatigue. Current regimen was reviewed and appears to be providing partial benefit without reported adverse effects. Will continue present therapy for now, monitor symptom response, and adjust treatment if symptom control becomes inadequate or side effects emerge.

## Build logic recommendations

When generating the Assessment Summary paragraph, prioritize:

* reason for visit
* most clinically significant problems addressed
* symptom patterns
* meaningful lab abnormalities or trends
* response to treatment
* risk factors or monitoring needs

When generating each problem-based Assessment/Plan item:

* use problem-specific context from the encounter
* mention labs only if relevant
* mention treatment response only if known
* mention monitoring/follow-up only if appropriate
* avoid repeating the exact same phrasing across diagnoses

## Optional enhancement

If multiple related problems are discussed (for example insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, central adiposity, elevated hs-CRP), allow the assessment wording to reflect the broader metabolic pattern rather than treating every issue as a completely isolated one.

Likewise, if symptoms and hormone patterns fit together, allow the note to sound integrated and clinically synthesized.

## Bottom line

Please revise the SOAP note generator so the Assessment & Plan feels more like a real, medically thoughtful outpatient note with concise clinical reasoning and a summary paragraph at the start of the Assessment section, while remaining strictly faithful to the actual encounter data.
